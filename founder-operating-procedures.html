Introduction
Welcome, Ja'Nae
Everything you need to run the Allegiance Founder Exit Planning process — from first contact to full planning package.
What Allegiance Partners does: We are a boutique Multi Family Office focused on founders approaching a major business exit. Our clients are typically minority and women founders, many coming through the NYC EDC Founder Fellowship and Allegiance Pathway. We help them understand — often for the first time — what their exit will look like after tax, what planning structures are available to them, and how to act before it is too late. We then serve as their Multi Family Office after the exit: coordinating their investment management, estate planning, tax planning, and philanthropy in one integrated relationship.

This manual describes the full process — from the moment a founder first comes onto our radar, through the Discovery Call, to the delivery of their personalised planning package. It is written so that you can eventually run this process independently, with John available for strategy and client relationship decisions.

A note on tone: Founders are under enormous pressure. They are trying to build a business, manage investors, hire people, and run their lives — all at once. Our job is to make planning feel empowering and clear, not complex and alarming. We never use the word urgent. We say first priority. We never pile on. We show them the opportunity, not the problem.

What makes Allegiance different

Most wealth advisors wait for a founder to come to them after the exit, by which point much of the tax planning opportunity has already closed. We engage before the exit — ideally 3-5 years out — and use that time to put structures in place that can save millions of dollars. The Founder Exit Toolkit is our calling card: an interactive, personalised planning tool that shows a founder the shape of their plan in real time. No other advisor in our space is doing this.

Section 2
Pre-Discovery Call
What to prepare before you get on the call.

Research the founder

Before every Discovery Call, spend 20-30 minutes researching the founder. The goal is to arrive knowing as much as possible so the conversation feels like a continuation of a relationship, not a cold intake.

  • LinkedIn — confirm their role, co-founders, company history, education
  • Crunchbase — funding rounds, investors, current valuation estimates
  • Company website — product, sector, team, any press coverage
  • NYC EDC / Newlab / Allegiance Pathway — how they came to us, any prior relationship context John has shared

Pre-fill the Intake Form

Open the Intake Form in Chrome before the call and fill in everything you already know. This typically includes:

  • Founder name, state, city
  • Company name, entity type, year incorporated
  • Raises to date (from Crunchbase)
  • Current implied valuation (if known)
  • Co-founder name (if applicable)
Pre-filling makes the live part of the call feel fluid and professional. The founder sees you already know their story — you're asking the questions you don't already know the answers to.

Set up screen sharing

On Google Meet, use "Share this tab" (not "Share your whole screen"). This keeps the session clean — the founder sees only the Intake Form, not your desktop, other tabs, or email. In Chrome: click the screen share icon → select "A tab" → choose the Intake Form tab.

Check the Jane Toolkit was sent

If the founder hasn't received the Jane Toolkit yet, send it before the call with the standard cover email. It sets the context and means the founder arrives already curious about what their own numbers might look like.

Section 3
The Discovery Call
How to run the 45-60 minute intake session.
Discovery Call — Flow

Opening the call

Start by sharing your screen and showing the founder the Intake Form. Type their first name immediately — the preview panel title changes to "★ [Name]'s Planning Preview" and the four boxes appear. This sets the tone: this is about them, not about us.

Opening line suggestion: "I'm going to fill this in as we talk — and you'll see your plan taking shape in real time. By the end of the call we'll have a clear picture of what your exit could look like." That's all you need to say. The form does the rest.

Walking through the sections

Open each section as the conversation naturally reaches that topic. Don't rush — the collapsible sections let you keep earlier parts visible while you move through the form. The order that usually works best:

  1. Section 1 (Founder) — confirm personal details, ask about family, gently raise the estate plan question
  2. Section 2 (Company) — company name, entity type (critical for QSBS), raises, current valuation
  3. Section 5 (Exit) — target valuation, timeline. This is often the most energising part of the conversation
  4. Section 4 (Future Ownership) — four buckets: Founder / Investors / Family / Charity. Keep it simple
  5. Section 7 (Tax) — let the auto-fill do the work. Just confirm state and NYC residency
  6. Sections 8-9 (Advisors / Notes) — who do they have, who do they need

The estate plan question

Section 1 includes a three-option estate plan question. Here is how to handle each answer:

AnswerWhat it meansWhat you say
No planMost common. No legal protection for their assets or wishes."The first thing we'd want to do is get a basic estate plan in place — a Will, Power of Attorney, and Healthcare Proxy. It's the foundation everything else rests on, and it doesn't take long. We'll coordinate that with a T&E attorney."
Basic planSomething exists, but probably outdated and not built for a major exit."Good — it would be worth a review to make sure it reflects where the business is heading. Plans done a few years ago rarely anticipate an exit at this scale. We'd coordinate that review with your attorney."
ComprehensiveRare at this stage. Even so, it almost certainly needs updating for the exit."That's great — we'd want to integrate it with the exit planning to make sure everything is aligned. There are usually a few gaps when a liquidity event of this size comes into view."

The live preview — let it breathe

When you fill in the Exit Valuation and the Future Ownership buckets, the four boxes update immediately. Don't talk over this moment. Let the founder look at the numbers. They will often say something — that's the opening for the planning conversation.

Section 4
Post-Call: Building the Toolkit
From constants file to personalised Toolkit — same day.

What the Toolkit is

The Toolkit is a single interactive HTML file, pre-loaded with the founder's numbers. It has four or five tabs depending on the founder's profile:

  • Roadmap — phase-by-phase planning milestones with checkboxes and tags (Legal / Tax / Trust / MFO)
  • Exit Scenarios — sliders for exit valuation, ownership split, and tax rate. Four boxes update in real time.
  • QSBS — only if the entity is a C-Corp. Shows the stacking opportunity across vehicles.
  • Post-Exit Tax Planning — PPLI/FCV calculator for larger exits ($36M+ in trust)
  • Trust Structure — Dynasty Trust or Multi-Gen Trust decision framework
  • Advisor Team — six advisor cards showing the coordinated team

Section 6
Key Planning Rules
The non-negotiables. Learn these before anything else.
These rules apply in every founder package, without exception. They exist because tax and estate planning mistakes are expensive and sometimes irreversible. When in doubt, ask John before proceeding.
RuleThe DetailStatus
QSBS: C-Corp only §1202 QSBS exclusion applies only to C-Corporations. S-Corps, LLCs, and partnerships do not qualify. If the entity is not a C-Corp, the QSBS tab is absent from the Toolkit and QSBS is set to $0. The Intake Form auto-flags this. Hard rule
QSBS: confirm with CPA Even for C-Corps, QSBS eligibility must be confirmed with the founder's CPA before any planning is implemented. We never say "you qualify for QSBS" — we say "your CPA should confirm eligibility." The key questions: 80%+ of assets in a qualifying trade or business? Gross assets under $50M at issuance? Hard rule
DAF: always pre-LOI The DAF charitable gift of shares must happen before any Letter of Intent is signed. Once the LOI is signed, the IRS may treat the income as already "assigned" to the founder — killing the deduction. This is the assignment of income doctrine. There is no workaround. Coordinate timing with estate counsel and M&A counsel. Hard rule
Services Agreement: verbal only The Allegiance Family Office Services Agreement is never mentioned in any written deliverable — not the One-Pager, not the Road Map, not the Appendices. John introduces it verbally at the right moment in the relationship. Do not include it in any document. Hard rule
No "urgent" language We never use the word "urgent" in any client-facing document or email. Founders are under enough pressure. We use "first priority" instead. This applies to the Toolkit, Road Map, One-Pager, send notes, and all emails. Hard rule
Trust zeroed: note it, preserve optionality If a founder's personal circumstances mean the Dynasty Trust is not appropriate now (e.g. single, no children, no immediate family plans), the trust allocation is zeroed — but the optionality is preserved and noted. The trust can be established later if circumstances change; the QSBS date tacks. Judgement call
NYC vs NJ comparison: omit for solidly NYC founders We do not include the NYC vs NJ tax rate comparison in documents for founders who are clearly and permanently NYC-based. It is irrelevant to them and potentially distracting. Judgement call
Appendices: generic throughout All appendices refer to "the Founder" — never the founder's actual name, specific numbers, or exit timeline. Personalisation is reserved for the Road Map, IFM, Toolkit, and One-Pager only. Hard rule

Section 8
QSBS Decision Tree
§1202 Qualified Small Business Stock — how to think about it.

What is QSBS?

Section 1202 of the Internal Revenue Code allows a taxpayer to exclude up to $10 million of capital gain — or 10× their adjusted basis, whichever is greater — from the sale of Qualified Small Business Stock held for more than 5 years. Both federal and state tax apply to the excluded amount (for conforming states). At a 38.576% NYC blended rate, a $10M exclusion saves approximately $3.86M.

QSBS Eligibility — Decision Tree

QSBS stacking — why it matters

Each separate taxpayer can claim their own $10M QSBS exclusion. A non-grantor Dynasty Trust or Multi-Generational Trust is a separate taxpayer — so it qualifies for its own exclusion independently of the founder. This means:

VehicleExclusionCondition
Founder (individual)$10,000,000Standard §1202
Spouse / Co-founder$10,000,000Separate original issuance
Non-grantor Trust$10,000,000§1202(h)(2) tacks holding date
CRUT (optional)$10,000,000Non-grantor trust — optional vehicle
Base total (Founder + Trust)$20,000,000Primary planning assumption

Section 12
Glossary
Key terms you will encounter in this work.
TermWhat it means
QSBS (§1202)Qualified Small Business Stock. A federal tax exclusion of up to $10M of capital gain on C-Corp stock held for 5+ years. One of the most powerful tax planning tools available to founders. C-Corp only. CPA to confirm eligibility.
DAFDonor Advised Fund. A charitable giving account where the founder donates appreciated stock, receives an immediate tax deduction, and then directs grants over time. Must be funded before any LOI is signed.
Dynasty TrustAn irrevocable trust designed to hold assets across multiple generations without estate tax at each transfer. Delaware situs (no RAP, no income tax). Qualifies for own QSBS exclusion as a non-grantor trust.
Multi-Gen Trust (MGT)Similar to a Dynasty Trust but structured with sub-trust shares for each family branch. Used for founders with more complex family situations (e.g. PMW — 5 nieces/nephew).
NIITNet Investment Income Tax — a 3.8% ACA surcharge on investment income above certain thresholds. Applies to capital gains from a business exit.
Blended rateThe combined effective tax rate on a capital gain exit: Federal (20%) + NIIT (3.8%) + State + City. The number we use in all financial modelling.
LOILetter of Intent. The document that kicks off the formal sale process. All charitable gifting (DAF, trust) must happen before the LOI is signed — the assignment of income doctrine applies after.
MFOMulti Family Office. A firm that provides comprehensive wealth management services — investment management, tax, estate, philanthropy — to multiple high-net-worth families. What Allegiance Partners is.
GSTGeneration-Skipping Transfer tax. A federal tax on transfers that skip a generation (e.g. grandparent to grandchild). The GST exemption ($15M single, $30M married in 2026) can be allocated to a Dynasty Trust at creation, sheltering all future appreciation from GST at every generational transition.
Grantor TrustA trust where the founder pays income tax on trust earnings personally. Each tax payment is effectively an additional tax-free gift to the trust. Good for compounding but reduces QSBS stacking potential.
Non-Grantor TrustA trust that is a separate taxpayer — it files its own tax return and pays its own income tax. Qualifies for its own QSBS exclusion. Recommended for most Allegiance founders.
PPLIPrivate Placement Life Insurance. An insurance wrapper that allows investments to grow permanently tax-exempt inside an irrevocable trust. Relevant for post-exit trust proceeds of $20M+.
HEMSHealth, Education, Maintenance and Support — the standard distribution criteria for a trust. Allows the trustee to make distributions for these purposes without creating estate tax inclusion.
AddeparA portfolio reporting platform used by Allegiance to provide consolidated reporting across all of a client's accounts — personal, trust, DAF, banking — in a single dashboard.
SLAT / SLANTSpousal Lifetime Access Trust / Spousal Lifetime Access Non-Grantor Trust. Irrevocable trusts that include the spouse as a beneficiary. SLANT preserves QSBS stacking; SLAT is simpler. Relevant for married founders.
Constants fileAllegiance's internal term for the Python file ([name]_constants.py) generated by the Intake Form. Contains every key parameter for a founder — tax rates, ownership split, exit valuation, QSBS status. The input to all document builds.
